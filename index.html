The Big Ones

Work that defined the way I approach data problems. One born from volcanic ash, another from a community's need for esports analytics after official tools vanished.

  1. Finished

    Volcanic Recovery Analysis

    Used Sentinel imagery, NDVI indices, DEM data, and time-series clustering to study vegetation recovery on La Palma after the 2021 eruption. Also mapped lava-flow extent and recovery zones.

    I began this project after discovering the Sentinel satellite missions and wanting to go beyond surface-level tutorials. Working with raw scenes pushed me to learn rasterio workflows from scratch. A particularly rewarding challenge came when I introduced DEM data: different formats, mismatched resolutions, and alignment issues made integration tricky. Building a reprojection workflow and finally seeing vegetation indices plotted in 3D alongside elevation was amazing and made the work more than worth it.

    Stack Python · pandas · numpy · scikit-learn · rasterio · geopandas

    View repository

    Rather than repeating the usual "first remote sensing projects," I looked for something dramatic and meaningful. I landed on the 2021 La Palma volcanic eruption.

    Starting with raw Sentinel imagery, I dove into rasterio and other geospatial tools, experimenting with indices like NDVI, and gradually shaping the project into a structured analysis. Building a reprojection workflow and finally seeing vegetation indices plotted in 3D alongside elevation was a breakthrough moment.

    Beyond clustering vegetation "fingerprints," the toolset also lays the groundwork for mapping lava flows and comparing recovery across impact zones. It's not a formal paper, but a foundation others can build on—and it took me from zero geospatial experience to a working ecological workflow.

  2. Replaced by owtv.gg

    Overwatch Esports Analysis App

    Shiny app transforming community spreadsheets into a relational database with dashboards, map and hero analysis, plus ML predictions using random forest, boosted trees, and logistic regression.

    When Blizzard sunset the official Overwatch League stats portal, I decided to build a community-friendly replacement. Scraping, cleaning, and modeling transformed fan-maintained sheets into live dashboards with map and hero breakdowns. The project became surprisingly impactful—streamers, coaches, and YouTubers used it; I even developed a small reputation in the esports community. While it never had the full polish of a professional product, my domain knowledge enabled insights that didn't exist elsewhere.

    Stack R · tidyverse · Shiny · tidymodels

    View repository

    I've played Overwatch since its release, and at times competitively, so I naturally became fascinated with the esports scene. During university, I discovered the Overwatch League's dedicated statistics site, only to see it shut down when the league ended. Its replacement, the Overwatch Champions Series (OWCS), lacked anything close to the depth of the original stats lab. As a data enthusiast, I was disappointed, but also inspired: if the community didn't have a proper analytics platform, I could build one.

    I started by scraping community spreadsheets and transforming them into a relational database on AWS. Using R and Shiny, I built dashboards, team and map breakdowns, hero ban and interaction analysis, and predictive modules using random forests, boosted trees, and logistic regression via tidymodels.

    The project became surprisingly impactful. Streamers, coaches, and YouTubers used it; I even developed a small reputation in the esports community. At one point, a professional coach reached out about my opinion on machine learning in the context of team composition setup. Beyond the technical challenge, this was my first experience of building something others genuinely relied on.

Lab Notes

  • Hot Moon Climate Simulator

    A 2D climate simulation of a fictional exoplanet moon, built to explore both numerical methods and climate physics. The core challenge was formulating continuous differential equations for energy balance, radiative transfer, and moisture dynamics, then solving them efficiently using Julia's DifferentialEquations.jl library. The simulation evolves temperature and precipitation across a 90x180 grid, classifies 12 biomes, and produces animated visualizations.

    This project served two purposes: learning to work with ODE solvers and large coupled systems, and building physical intuition for climate dynamics like ice-albedo feedback, rain shadows, and greenhouse effects. Both skills feed directly into my applied climate work.
