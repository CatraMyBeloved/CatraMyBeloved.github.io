Chapter 1

Why This Work

As many others do, I believe that climate change and our impact on the environment is one of the biggest challenges humanity is facing. In my opinion, if humanity is to continue, we will have to protect and conserve nature wherever we can. The idea of spending my life optimizing profit margins for a company doesn't interest me. I want to contribute to science for science's sake, deepen our understanding of the world because we can and are curious.

I myself am endlessly curious about how the world works. I lose myself in Wikipedia rabbit holes, or write climate simulations because I always wanted to learn how ODE solvers work. All of these topics are fascinating to me, and I love sharing that with others. I spend at least a little time every day with friends, be it online or in person.

I believe that the world should be better than it is right now, and I think the best I can help with that is through what I'm good at: Data Science.

I want to be as kind and open at work as I am with my friends. I want to care about people, and show that openly.

Chapter 2

The Discovery

In university, I took an introductory Python course. It clicked immediately for me. Programming just made sense, I could see it all inside of my head. We had a brief section about data analysis, and I found it absolutely fascinating. The math, the statistics, the models, I love all of it. Finding stuff that was invisible before.

I've played Overwatch since its release in 2016, sometimes competitively. I knew about the Overwatch League (Overwatch's Esport) stats lab and talked to my professor about it. We designed a small task together for the rest of the course, and it was still part of the material when I left university.

This was my first real contact with "proper" analysis. Once I got around to playing with the actual data, I loved being able to show stuff like "the amount of healing doesn't matter statistically at all in this team composition, but matters a lot in this one." Feelings you had as a player, represented in the numbers.

Finding my passion in data analysis was huge for me, and I am still thankful to my professors for supporting that passion during my time in university.
Chapter 3

La Palma

After that first project, I wanted to try something more ambitious. I'm fascinated by almost all scientific disciplines, and a big fan of aerospace operations. Because of this, I decided to learn geospatial data analysis using satellite imagery. As a cool event to study, I chose the 2021 La Palma volcanic eruption. Something relevant to many disciplines, and where there was actually something to see.

Volcanic Recovery Analysis

Sentinel imagery analysis of vegetation recovery patterns using NDVI indices, DEM data, and unsupervised clustering to map recovery zones.

Stack: Python · rasterio · geopandas · scikit-learn

View repository

I started with raw Sentinel satellite imagery and taught myself rasterio workflows from scratch through tutorials. The trickiest part was integrating DEM data: different formats, mismatched resolutions, alignment issues everywhere. Building that reprojection workflow piece by piece took a while, but finally seeing vegetation indices plotted in 3D alongside elevation was amazing.

Chapter 4

The Alps Project

Not everything worked out the way I wanted it to. My next plan was to study shifts in species abundance in the German Alps, a place I spent time as a kid on holidays, hiking from hut to hut. The idea was to correlate observation data from GBIF with climate variables from ERA5. I couldn't find any papers doing what I had in mind, so it would have been genuinely new.

I got the data, but normalization killed the project. The number of observations reported overall kept growing year over year, so it was hard to tell if a species was actually declining or just being reported less. The Alps aren't densely populated either, so there was a lot of variance. I tried adjusting for local observation growth, general growth, number of independent contributors. Nothing worked cleanly. Species that should have been stable according to literature varied wildly in the data. Declining ones stayed flat.

It was my second project ever. I'd love to revisit it someday with better methods.

Chapter 5

Toward Research

I've known for a while that I want to work at a public research institute. Contributing to humanity's collected understanding of the world around us is a dream of mine. Their missions are exactly what I would want to work towards, at places like the AWI (Alfred Wegener Institute) or the Helmholtz Center.

In 2024, I was accepted for an internship with the data science support group at the AWI. I couldn't attend because it conflicted with my exams and study plans at the time. I didn't know yet that I would drop out, but that conversation confirmed my passion for this field. I still regret not taking the opportunity, but I hope to have another chance at scientific work in the future.
